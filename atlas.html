The Problem

VC analysts spend ~118 hours per potential investment. Relationship data is scattered across email, LinkedIn, and notes. There's no systematic way to prioritize deal flow. Teams spend more time managing information than making decisions -- and the best opportunities get lost in the noise.

The Insight

Three signals reveal the true trajectory of a startup: revenue growth, total users acquired, and founders' inter-relationships. If a platform could automatically surface these signals and map the relationship layer, analysts could cut evaluation time from 60 minutes to 15 -- and make better decisions doing it.

The Solution

Atlas is the operating system for venture teams -- combining an agentic AI system, relationship mapping engine, and startup scoring into one platform. It connects LinkedIn, GitHub, Google Scholar, and email to give analysts a structured, focused workflow.

Reflection
What I Learned
The relationship mapping angle was the most differentiated part of Atlas and also the hardest to build conviction around early. Most VC tools focus on documents and data -- the network layer is underbuilt. That's where the real insight was, and I'm proud we leaned into it even when it was the harder thing to explain.
What I'd Do Differently
I'd have done more primary research with actual analysts before the competition. We built personas based on public information, but a few 30-minute calls would have sharpened our understanding of where the real friction is in the day-to-day workflow.
Grateful For
Product Heist pushed me to think beyond features and into business models and go-to-market. I'm grateful for a competition format that asked us to think like founders, not just product managers.
